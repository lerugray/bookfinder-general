# The Macedonian Phalanx (Taylor) — Chapter & Section Index

**EPUB ORDER WARNING:** the extracted `content.md` is badly scrambled (file order ≠ reading order). The book's *intended* order (from the Contents block) is below. Line numbers cite `content.md`.

## Introduction / Note on sources (L6563)
Frames the project as the rise, dominance and decline of the Macedonian phalanx. Stresses the patchy evidence: only Polybius is a contemporary historian of the Hellenistic phalanx, and the surviving drill manuals (the "tacticians") all post-date Alexander by 200–300 years. Running caution: don't assume detail recorded c. AD 100 applies unchanged to Philip and Alexander.

## Chapter 1 — Origins (L6182)
Traces the phalanx's invention to Philip II's reforms c. 359–358 BC (Diodorus 16.3.1–2: Philip "devised the compact order... imitating the 'locked shields' of the warriors at Troy"). Lays out the orthodox "distinctive features" list (close order, long two-handed sarissa, smaller *pelte* shield, high drill, ponderousness) that Taylor spends the book testing.

## Chapter 2 — Arms and armour (L7318)
Centerpiece is **the sarissa**: Taylor refuses a single "true" length, giving a chronological series — Theophrastus' 12-cubit, Polyaenus' 16-cubit, Polybius 18.29's 16-cubit design "reduced in practice to fourteen," of which 4 are gripped, leaving 10 projecting (L7479). Key model: the **five projecting rank-points** (rank spacing 2 cubits, so ranks 1–5 all reach beyond the front "by a descending scale of two cubits"). Also catalogs shields (*aspis*/*pelte*), cuirass, helmet, sword.

## Chapter 3 — Organization and drill (L10052) *(the structural core)*
Defends the reliability of the tacticians (Asclepiodotus earliest, ~100 BC). Establishes the **file as the building block** (16 deep standard, 8 under Alexander when called a *dekad*), the three **intervals** (open 4 cubits / close 2 cubits / *synaspismos* 1 cubit, L10204), and the doubling **unit ladder**: file → *syntagma* (256 = 16×16) → *chiliarchy* (1,024) → *taxis* (~1,500 under Alexander). Taylor's own view: there was never one exact interval — formations were spaced by eye, not a measuring stick (L10245).

## Chapter 4 — The men in the phalanx (L6629)
The phalanx as a social/political institution. The phalangite was a "citizen soldier" (in Macedon the *Makedones*); the Successor kingdoms recreated this class artificially (Ptolemaic **cleruchy** land-grants), a drift Taylor links to the manpower crisis before Raphia (217).

## Chapter 5 — Manpower and recruitment (L5507)
How each kingdom kept the phalanx supplied with bodies. The prized "Macedonian" identity was conferred by *armament*, not always ancestry — the Seleucid phalanx was likely mixed Greek stock "armed in the Macedonian fashion." Documents military "pseudo-ethnics."

## Chapter 6 — Command and control (L4710)
Distinguishes the "King, Friends and military forces" structure. Maps the officer hierarchy onto the unit ladder (file-leaders up to *taxis* commanders). Key point: rank did not carry fixed authority — a "taxiarch" simply commanded a *taxis* (L4742).

## Chapter 7 — Battles and tactics (L8541)
What manoeuvres the phalanx could perform. Armies marched in **column** and deployed into line near the field. Introduces the doubled formations (*diphalangia*, *tetraphalangia*). Pushes back on Polybius' claim that the phalanx was fit for only one kind of fighting.

## Chapter 8 — Fighting in the phalanx (L12981)
The "face of battle" chapter. Surveys the *othismos* ("rugby-scrum" shoving) controversy — Taylor is a dissenter (L13044). Recaps the physical reality: hoplite ~8 deep with a 2 m one-handed spear; the Macedonian phalangite 16 (up to 32) deep, drilled intervals, sarissa "typically 6 or 7 metres... wielded probably underarm in two hands" (L13025). Uses Early Modern pike-block evidence as an analogue.

## Chapter 9 — Legion and phalanx (L3303) *(the analytical payoff)*
Why Rome won. Summarizes **Polybius 18.28–32**: each Roman in open order faced 2 front-rank Macedonians plus the points behind (≈10 sarissae), so the phalanx was unbeatable frontally on level ground — but Rome could refuse battle on the phalanx's terrain, or exploit the gaps that open in a single continuous line. Taylor's amendment: the decisive factor was less terrain (overstated) than the **grand-tactical flexibility of the triple Roman line** with independent maniples/reserves (L3349). Cynoscephalae, Magnesia, Pydna.

## Epilogue (L6105)
The pike idea outlived the legion: Byzantine drill, the Scottish schiltron, Swiss/Italian pike blocks; Early Modern armies fused the two systems — "armed like Macedonians, but organized on the grand tactical scale like Romans" — until musket-plus-bayonet retired the pike c. 1700.
