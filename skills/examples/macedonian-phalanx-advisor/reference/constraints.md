# Tactical Framework — The Macedonian Phalanx

**Source:** Taylor, *The Macedonian Phalanx: Equipment, Organization and Tactics*. Line citations refer to `content.md` in this folder.

How the phalanx actually fought, and the hard limits a wargame designer should encode. Each principle is stated as **the limit is X because Y**, with the mechanism and the quantitative anchor where the text gives one.

---

## 1. It fights as a block, not as men. The limit is: no individual weapon-play.

**Claim.** The defining feature of the Macedonian phalanx is that it "did not require individual weapons play but instead depended on the mass effect of the whole formation" (L6115). The combat power output is a property of the *formation*, not of the soldiers in it.

**Mechanism.** The sarissa is a two-handed pike held near its rear end (L7479, L7497). A man so armed cannot fence, parry, or pick targets — the weapon is too long to wield, and both hands are committed. The author repeatedly insists the sarissa was "pressed into an opponent's shield… by the force of sixteen men," not thrust by the arms (L3160). The front-rank man is a delivery point for the pressure of the whole file behind him. Diodorus traces this to Philip deliberately imitating the Homeric "locked shields," devising "the compact order" through "constant manoeuvres… and competitive drills" (L6333).

**Design anchor.** Model the phalanx unit as the atom of combat resolution. There is no per-figure skill check, no duel, no "elite swordsman" modifier inside the block. Quality lives in **drill, cohesion, and depth**, not in individual stats. A high-skill individual inside an intact phalanx contributes nothing he could not contribute as filler; the same man's skill only matters once the formation breaks and he is "fought man to man or in small detachments" (L3778) — at which point the sarissa is useless and he is reduced to hacking "with their small daggers against the firm shields of the Romans" (L3778). This is the inversion the designer must capture: **inside the block, mass beats skill; outside it, the phalangite is the worst heavy infantryman on the field.**

## 2. The sarissa "hedge": ~5 points project from every front-rank man. The limit is: five fighting ranks, no more.

**Claim.** Multiple front ranks present their pikes simultaneously, so the enemy meets a wall of points before reaching the front-rank man.

**Mechanism and the numbers.** All four tacticians agree on the geometry, with minor variation (L7479, L7490, L7497). Taking Polybius (18.29, L7479): a 14-cubit sarissa, 4 cubits taken by the grip and rear balance, leaves 10 cubits projecting forward. Ranks stand 2 cubits apart. So:

- Rank 1 projects 10 cubits beyond the front
- Rank 2 projects 8
- Rank 3 projects 6
- Rank 4 projects 4
- Rank 5 projects 2

"In front of each man of the front rank there will be five sarissae projecting" (L7479) — "to distances varying by a descending scale of two cubits." Arrian and Aelian, with a 16- or 14-cubit weapon held without rear projection, get **six** ranks projecting (L7490, L7522); Asclepiodotus's 12-cubit weapon gets five (L7485). So the anchor is **5 points per front-man, occasionally 6.**

**Frontal density.** Combine with the frontage rule (§5). At the standard 2-cubit (1 metre) file interval, each metre of front carries one front-rank man presenting his own point plus four-to-five more from behind. Polybius's headline figure: because the Macedonian occupies half the Roman's frontage, "each Roman had to fight ten Macedonians" — two front-rankers and "the pike points of a further eight behind" when the phalanx is in closest order (L3331, L10210). The hedge is why the phalanx is "irresistible" head-on: an enemy "hurling their javelins to no purpose" then drawing swords simply cannot reach a body to strike (L3560, L3832).

**Design anchor.** Frontal attack into an intact phalanx should be near-suicidal: the attacker is stopped at pike-length and takes attrition without dealing any. Encode the hedge as a fixed **5-point (max 6) frontal projection** that the enemy must defeat or bypass, never out-fight man-to-man. Ranks beyond the fifth/sixth add nothing to the points presented forward.

## 3. Depth beyond the fighting ranks buys push, morale, and replacement — never reach or frontage. The limit is: rank 6+ does not fight.

**Claim.** A 16-deep file has only 5–6 ranks whose pikes reach the enemy. The other 10+ ranks cannot strike and do not widen the line. So why field them?

**Mechanism.** Three returns on extra depth, none of them offensive reach:

1. **Physical push ("weight").** The phalanx's "weight" explicitly "does not mean its literal weight but refers to the deep and dense formation, which allowed the phalanx to advance and drive back its opponents" (L3509). At Cynoscephalae Philip's right won because it was "superior in the weight of their formation" (L3506); at Sellasia the Spartans were "pushed from their ground by the weight of the Macedonian phalanx" (L8857). The author connects this to the Early-Modern "push of pike," where "the deep units sought to surge over the enemy and press him down" (L3186). Rear ranks lean their mass into the men ahead.
2. **Morale and steadiness.** Greater depth gives "greater stability" (L8844). At Magnesia Antiochus deployed an exceptional 32 ranks (double normal) precisely so a "deep, dense and terrifying block" could break the centre (L3611, L8841). Depth is what lets a unit absorb being driven back without breaking — Sellasia shows the Macedonians "gradually fell back facing the enemy… for a long distance" yet held (L8857).
3. **Replacement.** The best men are deliberately placed at the **front (file leaders)** and **rear (file closers)** of each file (L4195, L4207). File leaders are "specially selected for courage and experience, and receiving higher pay"; the file closer keeps "the men in their ranks and prevent[s] any hesitation or running away" and brings up stragglers (L4195, L5203). The intervening ranks are the file's body — filler and reserve of bodies to feed forward as front-rankers fall.

**Quantitative anchor.** Standard depth is **16** (L6526, L6517); occasionally doubled to **32** in confined frontage (Magnesia, Sellasia, Atrax; L3611, L8841). Hoplite norm was 8. Only **5–6** of those ranks ever reach the enemy with a point.

**Design anchor.** Split depth from frontage cleanly. Depth contributes a **push/shove value** (forward pressure on contact), a **morale/stability pool** (resistance to recoil and rout), and a **casualty buffer** (front-rank losses replaced from behind at no loss of frontage) — and contributes **nothing** to reach or to the number of attacks delivered. Doubling depth halves frontage at the same manpower; it does not double output. A deep phalanx on cramped ground is a legitimate, deliberate choice (fewer places to open a gap — §4), not a mistake.

## 4. Fatal vulnerability: flanks, rear, and any gap. The limit is: it can only fight forward, as one unbroken line.

**Claim.** The phalanx is a single continuous line that can engage only to its front. Anything that gets onto its flank, into its rear, or into a gap in the line wins.

**Mechanism — flanks and rear.** "It is impossible for the phalanx to turn right about face or to fight man to man" (L3525). At Cynoscephalae an unnamed tribune took 20 maniples, wheeled, "and thus getting behind the Macedonians… fell upon them in the rear" (L3519); the Macedonians, "incapable of protecting themselves," threw away their shields and fled (L3525). The rear attack's effect is primarily **morale**: it "had a tremendous morale impact… and exacerbated a formation's natural tendency to run away from the rear" — the phalanx broke before being defeated in combat (L3531). The phalangite's pike and tiny shield give him no answer to a blow from the side "where their arms did not shield them" (L3778).

**Mechanism — gaps.** The phalanx "fought as a single continuous line… and relied on winning a single decisive combat" (L3346). It is a "one-shot weapon — either it prevailed or it was defeated for a generation" (L3535), with no reserves (L9353). Polybius's recipe: charge it head-on with only part of your force, let it drive your front back or chase your retreat; "in either case its peculiar order is dislocated," gaps open, and your reserves "fall upon them on their flank and rear" (L3340). The line cannot stay continuous because different sections advance at different speeds.

**Mechanism — terrain.** The phalanx needs "a country flat, bare, and without such impediments as ditches, cavities, depressions, steep banks, or beds of rivers: for all such obstacles are sufficient to hinder and dislocate this particular formation" (L3334, restated L8896). Rough ground forces gaps mechanically. Taylor's own corrective: the phalanx was *not incapable* on broken ground, "but it was at a disadvantage doing so, especially against a more flexible opponent" (L3343) — and at the decisive defeats, terrain mattered *less* than the self-inflicted gaps.

**The two case studies.**
- **Cynoscephalae:** a meeting engagement; the left met the Romans still in marching column and collapsed before contact, while the victorious right was taken in the rear (L3503, L3519).
- **Pydna:** "conditions were highly favourable for the phalanx" and terrain was "not decisive" (L3795, L3788). The Peltasts advanced "so far and so fast that they lost contact with the Chalcaspides, leaving a large gap… into which the more manoeuvrable Romans marched" (L3746). Plutarch adds the ground "was uneven, and because of the length of the line… they could not maintain their locked shields"; the phalanx got "many breaks and gaps," and Aemilius ordered maniples to "plunge quickly into the gaps… not fighting a single battle against them all, but many separate and successive battles," striking flanks and rear (L3775, L3778). Livy's verdict: closed up it "is irresistible; but… some sort of uproar… on a flank or from the rear" throws it into "utter confusion" (L3772).

**Design anchor.** Three hard rules: (a) the phalanx attacks/defends only to its front arc; a flank or rear contact should apply a large combat penalty *and* a morale/break check, with rear contact most catastrophic. (b) Movement over rough/broken ground, rapid or uneven advance, or excessive frontage must generate **gaps** — internal flanks the enemy can exploit. (c) No reserves: once any segment breaks, model contagion down the line ("roll up the rest of their line and win the battle," L3348). The phalanx is the strongest unit in the game frontally on level ground, and the most brittle the instant order is lost.

## 5. Two modes: open-order fencing vs. close-order locked-shields advance. The limit is: the deadly mode is also the least mobile.

**Claim.** Like all pike formations, the phalanx has "two basic ways of fighting; a more tentative fencing with the pike in more open order, or a very close-order mass advance" (L6127). The sources' "locked shields" (*synaspismos*) and "projecting spears" (*probole*) signal the second mode (L6127, L3837).

**The three orders (Asclepiodotus 4.1, L10170).**
- **Open / march order:** 4 cubits (2 m) per man, every side. "The natural one," no special name; "used when we are marching the phalanx upon the enemy" (L10204).
- **Intermediate / close ("compact"):** 2 cubits (1 m). The standard battle interval (L8847).
- **Closest / locked shields (*synaspismos*):** 1 cubit (50 cm). "A forced formation… used when the enemy is marching upon us" (L10204) — the static defensive mode, shields overlapping.

**Mechanism.** Open order is for moving and for tentative pike-fencing. The mass advance closes the files up, lowers all the sarissas together (*probole*), and drives forward as a wall — this is the "irresistible" version that terrifies (Aemilius "had never seen a sight more fearful," L3725) and physically shoves the enemy back (§3). The 1-cubit *synaspismos* is the defensive brace, e.g. against cavalry: Peltasts crossing a river "stood firm with shields interlocked" and the Aetolian horse "were unable to effect anything" (L9472). Taylor's caution: the literary *synaspismos* often just means "closed up to advance," not a measured 1-cubit drill (L8848, L13605).

**Design anchor.** Give the phalanx an **order/spacing state** the player toggles, with a transition cost (drill time, vulnerability mid-change): open = mobile but weak in melee; close = standard battle power; locked = best frontal defence (especially vs. cavalry and missiles) but effectively immobile. The mass advance is the win condition on level ground; locked shields is the anvil. The enemy's whole game is to avoid meeting either head-on.

## 6. Combined arms: peltasts/hypaspists, cavalry on the wings, phalanx as anvil. The limit is: alone, the phalanx loses the campaign even when it wins the battle.

**Claim.** The phalanx is "a specialist pitched battle-winning force and less useful for all the marching, foraging, raiding and besieging that war involved" (L3586). It must be embedded in a combined-arms army.

**Mechanism — the elite flexible infantry.** The Hypaspists (Alexander) and their Antigonid successors the **Peltasts** are the "younger and elite element of the phalanx" (L6119), carrying shorter spears and smaller shields. Taylor's reading: a **dual-role** infantryman — "able to fight with short spear in more open order or full-length sarissa in the phalanx" (L7705) — used for scouting, seizing high ground, rapid marches, and forming the army's hinge. At Pydna they are the leading, fastest unit (L3702); their over-eager advance is also what opened the fatal gap (L3746). The Seleucid equivalent, the 10,000 Argyraspides, held a defile at Thermopylae as a "palisade" of pikes (L3556, L3553).

**Mechanism — cavalry and the anvil.** Taylor accepts the "hammer and anvil" only in a limited sense: "Alexander used his cavalry as a strike force, and… Hellenistic armies tended to open proceedings with cavalry engagements on the wings" (L3009). The phalanx fixes the enemy centre while cavalry decides the wings. Infantry-to-cavalry ratios fell from roughly 2:1 in Alexander's day to **8:1** in the late Antigonid army (L3009) — i.e. later armies leaned ever harder on the phalanx and lost the wing-decided cavalry battle, a structural weakness.

**The negative proof — Magnesia.** When the cavalry covering the phalanx's wings was driven off, "the Macedonian phalanx… when denuded of cavalry on either side" was surrounded by light cavalry, "had no opportunity to charge or even to deploy," and was shot to pieces from all sides by javelins and arrows "which missed not their mark in the dense mass." It withdrew in good order until panicking elephants inside the formation broke it (L3648). The anvil without its protecting arms is a stationary target.

**Design anchor.** Require the phalanx to be screened. Cavalry/light troops on the wings are not optional flavour — they are what keeps §4's flank/rear vulnerability from being exploited. Model an elite flexible-infantry unit (Hypaspist/Peltast) that can switch between skirmish/seize-ground and join-the-line roles. And at the campaign layer, the phalanx is a glass cannon: it wins set-piece battles but cannot raid, garrison, pursue, or replace losses ("a one-shot weapon," L3535) — the enemy who refuses pitched battle on level ground (Polybius, L3340) beats it without fighting it.

## 7. Command granularity: low-level drill, high-level by wing. The limit is: the general controls only his immediate vicinity.

**Claim.** The phalanx is extraordinarily drilled at the file/company level but crudely controlled at the army level.

**Mechanism — fine drill, coarse commands.** Drill orders are broken into atomic components: not "form close order to the right" but "right file hold position, other files right face, close up to the right, face the front, rear ranks advance" (L5295). Each file has a leader (front) and closer (rear) as its NCOs (L4207, L4195); each company carries supernumeraries — herald, signaller, bugler, aide, file-closer — to relay orders by voice, flag, and bugle through dust and din (L5203). A standard unit (*syntagma*) is only "16 metres on each side," so a sergeant-major's voice carries (L5293).

**Mechanism — the army moves in big lumps.** At battle scale, "large numbers of units are usually grouped together under a single officer, and orders issued to this officer; the king does not usually concern himself with the individual actions of separate units." Orders "only specify who, when or where to attack, or how, in general terms" — e.g. "a battle of wheeling tactics" (L5475). "Control in a battle was therefore limited to the commander's immediate vicinity, while other officers had to be entrusted to fight their own, separate battles on their own initiative" (L5492). No general could expect to cross from one flank to another mid-battle (L5496). Subordinate initiative cut both ways — at Pydna and Callinicus, sub-commanders launched the phalanx without the king's order (L3791).

**Design anchor.** Two-tier command. **Within a phalanx unit**, drilled state-changes (order, facing, depth-doubling, lock-shields) are reliable and fast — the unit executes complex manoeuvres. **Across the army**, the general issues coarse wing-level intents at deployment; once engaged, wings act semi-autonomously with limited mid-battle re-tasking, and the general's influence is local (a leadership/morale bonus only near his position). Initiative rolls for sub-commanders should be able to commit the phalanx prematurely — the very behaviour that opened the gaps at Cynoscephalae and Pydna.
