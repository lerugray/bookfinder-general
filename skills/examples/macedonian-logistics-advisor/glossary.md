# Engels, *Alexander the Great and the Logistics of the Macedonian Army* — Key Terms

A quick-reference glossary. Definitions are grounded in Engels' usage; where the user's term is a standard ancient unit Engels does not actually employ, that is flagged.

## Units of measure
- **choinix** — Engels' real grain unit: 1 *choinix* ≈ 1.92 pt. / 1.5 lb. of grain per day (Xerxes' ration); the Spartan ration was 4½ *choinices*. The book standardizes on 3 lb. food/day for men.
- **stade / stadia** — Greek distance unit Engels takes as roughly 1 *stade* ≈ 600 ft. (e.g., the Pyramus "one stade, about 600 feet wide"); he finds "no evidence for a short Macedonian stade." ~8.7 stades/mile.
- **parasang** — Persian distance unit Engels reckons at ~3 miles per parasang (used in his Xenophon-based route calculations).
- **talent** — unit of weight/treasure; the largest 5th-c. merchant ships carried 10,000 talents (~400 tons); 180,000 talents of treasure were stored at Ecbatana.
- **medimnus** — *(standard ancient dry measure, ~52 L of grain ≈ 48 choinices; Engels does NOT use this term — he reckons grain in choinices and pounds.)*
- **artaba** — *(Persian/Egyptian dry grain measure, ~30–55 L; not used by Engels — included only because it is a common ancient ration unit.)*
- **isohyet** — a line of equal rainfall; Engels' critical "10-inch (250 mm) isohyet, below which no perennial grasses can grow," marks the boundary between dry-farmable/grassy land and irrigation-only desert.

## Supply concepts
- **magazine / advance depot** — a pre-positioned store of grain (and sometimes water) established along the intended march route before the army arrives; essential in terrain lacking grain or water (e.g., Sinai, Pelusium).
- **requisition** — compulsory collection of supplies from a (usually surrendered/allied) local population, distinct from purchase or gift; one of three "friendly" acquisition modes (**gifts, requisition, market purchase**) used before the army reached hostile territory.
- **forage** — in Engels, the *grain* portion fed to animals (≈10 lb. grain/horse/day), often paired with —
- **fodder** — the bulk roughage portion (straw or chaff, ≈10 lb./horse/day); the horse ration is half grain (forage) + half fodder.
- **baggage train** — the column of pack animals (horses, mules, later camels — never oxen/donkeys, for speed/endurance) and few carts carrying the army's supplies and gear; Philip's reforms deliberately kept it small, the key to Macedonian mobility.
- **consumption rate** — the weight of food/water consumed per individual per day; constant regardless of army size, and the denominator against which carrying capacity is measured.
- **carrying-vs-consumption ratio** — the ~1:10 ratio of an animal's daily feed to the weight it can carry; fixed for any size of animal, it sets the hard ~4-day limit on desert crossings.
- **panoply** — a soldier's full set of arms and armor; Philip's innovation was making troops carry their own panoply plus provisions, eliminating servants/carts.
- **bematist** — a royal surveyor/pacer who measured march distances; the bematists' measurements (preserved in Pliny and Strabo) are Engels' "extremely accurate" primary evidence for route reconstruction (Table 8).

## Place / campaign terms that recur
- **Pinarus** — the river of the battle of Issus (334); Engels (App. 2) identifies it with the **Payas**.
- **Cilician Gates** — the strategic Taurus pass into Cilicia; its abandonment by the Persians was, per Alexander, one of his greatest strokes of luck.
- **Gedrosia / Gedrosian Desert** — the Makran desert of southern Baluchistan; the army's near-fatal march (325), planned around fleet resupply the monsoon prevented; capital at **Pura**.
- **Ichthophagi / Fish-eaters** — the coastal people of the Makran; the Gedrosians dwelt "inland and north" of them; their barren coast offered the fleet "not a single anchorage, well, or magazine."
- **Siwah** — the Libyan-desert oracle of Ammon; the 8-day expedition there is Engels' field test of the four-day-desert principle.
- **Sinai** — the desert crossing into Egypt requiring advance water depots; second only to Gedrosia in difficulty.
- **Companion cavalry (Hetairoi)** — Alexander's elite cavalry; with the Thessalian cavalry, often detached under Parmenio to guard the baggage train.
- **phalanx** — the Macedonian infantry body, "composed of peasants who could not afford expensive hoplite armor," whose light, self-carrying organization underpins the logistic system.
