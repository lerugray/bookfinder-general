The true table of contents (recovered from the book's own Contents page) is: Introduction; Ch. 1 *The Macedonian Army and Its Logistic System*; Ch. 2 *Greece and Turkey*; Ch. 3 *Syria, Lebanon, Israel, Egypt, and Iraq*; Ch. 4 *Iran and Afghanistan*; Ch. 5 *Uzbekistan, Tadzhikistan, Turkmenistan, Pakistan, and Southern Iran*; Ch. 6 *Conclusion*; Appendices 1–5.

## Introduction
States the thesis and method. Prior scholars (Tarn, Burn, Berve, Fuller) treated Macedonian supply as automatic or mysterious; Engels argues instead that "supply was the basis of Alexander's strategy" and that knowing a region's climate, geography, transport options, and harvest calendar lets you predict Alexander's next move. The two-part method: (1) build a mathematical model of the army's consumption vs. carrying capacity, then (2) apply it to the real human/physical geography of the route. **Lesson:** logistics is the hidden engine of the campaign — *"only a limited amount of supplies could be carried from one district to the next."*

## Chapter 1 — The Macedonian Army and Its Logistic System *(the methodological core)*
The quantitative heart of the book. Estimates troop/follower/animal numbers (one follower per three combatants before Gaugamela, one per two after), fixes consumption rates (3 lb. food + 2 qt. water per man/day; ~10 lb. grain + 10 lb. forage + 8 gal. water per horse), and derives the carrying-vs-consumption model. Key result: an army supplied only by men and pack animals *"cannot advance through desert where neither grain, fodder, or water is available for more than four days"* (line 2034). Also establishes that Philip's reforms (soldiers carry their own panoply + provisions; no carts/women; only horses/mules/camels) made this *"the fastest, lightest, and most mobile force in existence."* **Lesson:** the ratio of carrying capability to consumption is fixed (~1:10 for any animal regardless of size), so the army's range in barren terrain is a hard physical limit, not a matter of adding more animals.

## Chapter 2 — Greece and Turkey *(Macedonia / Asia Minor)*
The opening campaign of 334. Departure (probably from Therma, a port) timed so the army carries a 30-day grain supply to last until the June harvest; sea/river transport stressed as vastly cheaper than land (a merchant ship carries ~400 tons vs. 200 lb. per pack animal). Covers the coast-securing strategy against the Persian fleet, the Anatolian plateau crossing, and the lucky abandonment of the Cilician Gates. **Lesson:** the agricultural calendar dictates campaign timing — armies must wait out the pre-harvest "hungry months" or carry their own grain.

## Chapter 3 — Syria, Lebanon, Israel, Egypt, and Iraq *(the Levant / Egypt / Mesopotamia)*
From Issus (Nov. 334) down the Phoenician coast, the Sinai crossing, the Siwah oracle expedition, and the march up into Mesopotamia toward Gaugamela. The Sinai is flagged as *"the most arduous march undertaken by the Macedonian army until the Gedrosian Desert,"* requiring advance water depots. The Siwah trip is treated as a controlled test of the Ch. 1 four-day-desert principle. Explains Alexander's choice to march east (the well-watered, grassy Taurus/Zagros flank) rather than down the barren Euphrates — governed by the 10-inch isohyet below which no perennial grass grows. **Lesson:** advance magazines and the fleet make otherwise impassable coast and desert legs survivable; the isohyet line is a strategic boundary.

## Chapter 4 — Iran and Afghanistan *(Mesopotamia/Iran into the eastern campaign)*
From Babylon through Susa and Persepolis into the Iranian heartland and across the Hindu Kush. Marks the strategic pivot: once past the Niriz Basin, Alexander *"can no longer pose as a liberator,"* populations stop pre-surrendering, and supply shifts from gifts/requisition/markets to **pillage and foraging**. Introduces the divide-the-army tactic and absolute reliance on advance intelligence. **Lesson:** in hostile, un-surrendered territory the army must split into smaller units (smaller requirements are easier to procure) and never commit the whole force without prior intelligence on *"the roads, resources, terrain, and climate."*

## Chapter 5 — Uzbekistan, Tadzhikistan, Turkmenistan, Pakistan, and Southern Iran *(Bactria–Sogdia, India, and the Gedrosian desert)*
The most geographically uncertain chapter (no Sogdian site can be securely located, so reconstruction rests on logistic first principles). Covers Sogdia/Bactria, the irrigation-dependent settlement pattern, the descent of the Indus by a 2,000-vessel flotilla, and then the **Gedrosian catastrophe** — framed not as a blunder but as a meticulously planned coastal march meant to be provisioned by the fleet, wrecked when monsoon winds prevented the fleet from landing supplies. **Lesson:** even perfect planning fails when an uncontrollable variable (the monsoon, an unreachable fleet) breaks the supply chain; *"It was through the forces of nature, and not through a lack of knowledge ... that the expedition ended so catastrophically"* (line 3278).

## Chapter 6 — Conclusion
Synthesizes the argument. Philip's light baggage train is the decisive innovation; the two great constraints are primitive overland transport and subsistence-level ancient agriculture; the effective overland supply radius is only **60–80 miles**, forcing the army to winter only in populous, intensively cultivated places with river or sea access. Names the Persian Empire's terrain — deserts, salt wastes, mountains — as the king's *"most formidable weapon."* **Lesson:** *"Supply was indeed the basis of Alexander's strategy"* (line 3398).

## Appendix 1 — Rations
Justifies the consumption figures used throughout. Fixes the human ration at **3 lb. food/day** (cf. 1 *choinix* = 1.5 lb. for Xerxes' troops, up to 4½ for Spartans) and **2 qt. water/day** (after Maurice), arguing the water ration cannot be cut below 2 qt. in desert. Horse ration: ~20–32 lb. provisions/day (half grain, half fodder) + 8 gal. water. **Contributes:** the empirical numbers that drive the whole model.

## Appendix 2 — The Site of the Pinarus
A topographical/time-distance argument identifying the Pinarus (the Issus battlefield river) with the **Payas**, not the Deli Chai — placing the battle at the Deli Chai forces it to occur after dark, *"ignoring the laws of physics."* **Contributes:** a worked demonstration that the same time-distance-march-rate method also resolves a battlefield-location dispute.

## Appendix 3 — Approximate Chronology of the Gedrosian Campaign
A dated reconstruction: Alexander leaves Pattala mid-July 325, reaches the Kumbh Pass in late October, takes **60 days** to cover ~630–787 miles to Pura. **Contributes:** confirms Alexander's own four-months'-supply estimate was *"about correct"* — the campaign was timed, not improvised.

## Appendix 4 — Alexander's Route in the Gedrosian Desert
Locates the Gedrosians *"inland and north of the Fish-eaters [Ichthophagai],"* and notes that nowhere on the Ichthophagi coast was there *"a single anchorage, well, or magazine of provisions set up by the land army for the fleet"* (line 494) — the root cause of the disaster. **Contributes:** the physical-geography spine for Ch. 5's Gedrosia narrative.
